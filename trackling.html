RESEARCH

Understanding who we were designing for

To design for Gillian, we first had to understand her and thousands of seniors like her. Our team focused on adults aged 72+ whose daily routines and social exercise habits were heavily disrupted during COVID-19.

KEY FINDINGS

Through exploratory research, three user interviews, and synthesis on Miro, four insights emerged:

1. Pandemic restrictions removed the community that made seniors feel safe exercising
“Exercising with others is better than exercising alone… it's safer.” — User interview (Siu)
2. Falls are the leading cause of injury-related hospitalizations among seniors
“Different groups require different exercise strategies… tailored exercise prevents injury.” (Soni et al., 2015, Peel Public Health)
3. Fear of injury and uncertainty about ability prevent seniors from exercising independently
“Common barriers include fear of injury and lack of knowledge.” (Walther, 2015)
4. Without guidance, seniors struggle to know their limits or where to begin
“You wonder, what if something happens, maybe I shouldn’t be doing that, maybe that’s dangerous.” — Alice Herb (Kaiser Health News, 2021)

COMPETITOR ANALYSIS

Because walking is one of the safest forms of exercise for seniors, we analyzed three popular fitness apps to see how well they support older adults. While these apps track performance well, none are designed with seniors in mind.

OPPORTUNITY

Seniors weren't inactive because they didn't want to move — they were afraid. Our opportunity was to rebuild confidence by helping seniors understand their physical limits and exercise safely on their own.

IDEATION

Translating insights into early concepts

Based on our research, we explored three concepts to help seniors assess safety before activity, monitor exertion during exercise, and personalize future support based on user feedback.

Concept 1 – Pre-Walk Route Awareness

Helps seniors evaluate route difficulty and potential hazards before starting a walk.

Concept 2 – Live BPM Monitoring

Provides real-time heart rate feedback to prevent overexertion during exercise.

Concept 3 – Post-Activity Check-In

Collects feedback after exercise to adapt future workouts to the user’s abilities.

DESIGN & TESTING

Iterating toward the final design

Early concepts were developed into low-fidelity prototypes and evaluated through think-aloud usability testing with two seniors. Insights from testing guided refinements focused on accessibility, clarity, and ease of use.

Improving Onboarding Clarity
Before
Manual setup required users to enter personal information with no context about the app.
After
Clearer input outlines, optional health data syncing, and a brief app introduction guide users through setup.
Clarifying Route Information
Before
Icons lacked clear affordance, and route details were difficult to quickly scan.
After
Clearer visuals and a structured route preview make key information easier to digest.
Simplifying Post-Workout Check-in
Before
Check-in at the bottom felt secondary, with similar answer options and small checkbox-style inputs.
After
A dedicated survey screen with full-width selections and clear step progress makes it easier to complete.

Onboarding
Creates a personalized start by importing existing health data or guiding users through a simple manual setup.

Home & Navigation

The home page highlights daily steps and personalized activity insights, with bottom navigation to workouts, routes, tracking, and progress.

Live Tracking & Routes

Heart-rate tracking alerts users when exertion becomes unsafe, while nearby routes tailored to their abilities help plan safer walks.

Heart-rate zones based on American Heart Association: green (safe), yellow (caution), red (recover).

Post-Activity Check-In

A brief check-in helps Trackling adapt future routes and workouts to each user’s needs.

REFLECTION

Looking back

This project was my first time designing with real user research and working with complex health data for a target audience I had little personal connection to. While understanding accessibility principles was one thing, watching real users struggle made their importance immediate and tangible, really reshaping how I think about who I’m designing for.

If I were to revisit, I would prioritize earlier and more frequent testing. Due to COVID we were limited to two participants, which constrained how much we could have validated further. With broader access, I'd invest in deeper research into health data systems to make complex information feel simpler and more intuitive for seniors, while better integrating the product into their daily routines in a way that supports long-term confidence in staying active.
